A Review of Active Collaborative Perception and
Planning for Heterogeneous UAV-UGV Systems
Wenzhe Sun
Shien-Ming Wu School of Intelligent Engineering
South China University of Technology
Guangzhou, China
18865313659@163.com

Abstract—Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) and unmanned
ground vehicles (UGVs) offer complementary capabilities—widearea aerial surveillance versus high-payload ground operation—
making their coordination a compelling paradigm for complex
real-world missions. Yet deploying such heterogeneous systems
remains challenging owing to significant perceptual viewpoint
discrepancies, tightly coupled decision-making, and constrained
communication and computation budgets. Existing surveys address perception, planning, or control in isolation, leaving a critical gap in the systematic analysis of the full perception–planning–
decision–control chain. This paper presents a structured review of UAV-UGV collaborative systems, covering cooperative
architecture design, multi-source sensor fusion, active perception
strategies, multi-agent planning and decision-making, and motion
control. We further analyze key deployment bottlenecks under
dynamic real-world conditions and discuss how large language
models (LLMs) and embodied intelligence are poised to reshape
the architectural paradigm of future air-ground systems.
Index Terms—air-ground heterogeneous collaboration; UAVUGV; active perception; multi-agent reinforcement learning;
embodied intelligence

I. I NTRODUCTION
The complementary nature of UAVs and UGVs—aerial
breadth versus ground precision—has established air-ground
heterogeneous systems as a key enabler for missions ranging
from disaster response to precision agriculture. UAVs provide
rapid wide-area situational awareness but are limited in payload and endurance; UGVs deliver robust ground manipulation
and transportation yet suffer from terrain-constrained visibility.
Their integration enables a “global perception, local execution”
closed loop that neither platform can achieve alone [1].
Despite this promise, realizing efficient collaboration faces
three fundamental challenges. First, perceptual heterogeneity: UAV top-down imagery and UGV frontal LiDAR/depth
cameras differ substantially in scale, resolution, and geometry,
complicating data fusion and shared environment representation [2]. Second, active perception requirements: passive,
fixed-path sensing is insufficient; true intelligent coordination
demands that each agent autonomously plan optimal observation viewpoints to maximize information gain, imposing
tight perception-planning coupling [3]. Third, communication bandwidth constraints, real-time decision-making requirements under dynamic uncertainty, and the sim-to-real transfer
gap collectively hinder reliable field deployment [4].

A. Platform Dynamics and Kinematic Constraints
The core asymmetry between UAV and UGV stems from
their contrasting dynamics. A typical multi-rotor UAV enjoys 6-DOF agile motion governed by Newton–Euler equations [5], enabling hovering and rapid maneuvering. Most
UGVs, by contrast, are subject to nonholonomic constraints: differential-drive models prohibit lateral motion,
while Ackermann-steering models impose a minimum turning
radius [6]. This kinematic mismatch creates a hard physical
barrier for joint motion planning—3D UAV trajectories that
violate UGV nonholonomic constraints cannot be directly
executed—requiring cooperative planners to explicitly incorporate heterogeneous motion constraints into their formulations.
A notable special case is the tethered system [7], where
a physical cable connects UAV to UGV, enabling continuous
power supply and significantly extending UAV endurance. The
cable simultaneously introduces additional tension constraints
and collision-avoidance complexity, converting the relatively

Cooperative control architecture determines information
flow, decision mode, and overall system performance. Three
paradigms dominate current research. Centralized architectures aggregate all sensing data at a single compute node
for global planning, yielding theoretically optimal solutions
but suffering from poor scalability, heavy communication
overhead, and a single point of failure [8]. Decentralized
architectures grant each agent autonomous decision-making
from local observations, offering strong robustness and scalability, but local information may lead to suboptimal outcomes [9]. Hierarchical architectures balance the two by
decoupling high-level task allocation from low-level trajectory
tracking [6].
The rise of MARL has elevated Centralized Training with
Decentralized Execution (CTDE) to the dominant cooperative framework [9], [10]: global state is exploited during
training while each agent executes using only local observations at runtime. Value-decomposition algorithms exemplified
by QMIX [10] make this paradigm particularly effective for
tight cooperative tasks such as coordinated exploration and
target encirclement. The effectiveness of CTDE under partial
observability has been further validated in asynchronous multiagent settings [11], as illustrated in Fig. 2.

III. ACTIVE C OLLABORATIVE P ERCEPTION
A. Multi-Source Heterogeneous Sensor Fusion
The perceptual advantage of air-ground systems arises from
the complementarity of UAV top-down and UGV close-range
sensing, yet this same complementarity creates the primary
data fusion barrier [2]. Conventional geometry-based feature matching methods (SIFT, ORB) routinely fail on topdown/frontal image pairs due to sparse feature correspondence.
Research has consequently shifted toward deep learningbased adaptive feature fusion, where attention mechanisms
dynamically weight contributions from different viewpoints,
establishing cross-modal semantic associations without requiring precise pixel-level alignment.
At a higher level, Collaborative SLAM (Co-SLAM) enables UAV and UGV to independently build local submaps
subsequently merged via server-side global consistency optimization [12], providing heterogeneous agents with a shared,
accurate environmental representation that underpins the viewpoint planning in Section III-B.
B. Active Perception Strategies: From Information-Driven to
Task-Driven Viewpoint Planning
The field has evolved from model-based optimization
toward end-to-end learning. Classical information-theoretic
methods—frontier exploration and Next-Best-View (NBV)
planning [13]—maximize an information-gain function over
candidate viewpoints but depend on computationally expensive

ray-casting that limits real-time performance. Active Neural
SLAM [14] replaced hand-crafted heuristics with end-to-end
deep reinforcement learning, achieving continuous exploration
in highly dynamic environments.
Vutetakis et al. further proposed an active perception network that predicts information gain directly from local viewpoint features, entirely bypassing ray-casting and enabling
non-myopic online exploration [15]. For air-ground cooperative exploration, Dechicha et al. combined UAV-based widearea frontier scouting with UGV-based local NBV refinement [7], embodying an effective heterogeneous division of
labor: the UAV reduces global uncertainty while the UGV extracts fine-grained local detail. Despite their promise, learningbased policies remain sensitive to observation noise, motivating hybrid model-data approaches for deployment.
C. Cooperative Target Tracking
Cooperative target tracking exemplifies the closed-loop
synergy between a “sensing eye” (UAV) and an “executing
hand” (UGV). In the classical UAV-guided paradigm, the UAV
tracks targets from altitude and feeds interception waypoints to
the UGV in real time.Multi-agent reinforcement learning has
driven major advances: Munasinghe et al. demonstrated that
proficiency-constrained MARL yields significantly higher cooperative tracking success rates in dynamic vehicle pursuit [8];

A. From Explicit Optimization to Learned Decision-Making
Early methods relied on rule-based optimization. Market
auction mechanisms allocate tasks through distributed bidding
but provide no optimality guarantees [19]. MILP yields globally optimal solutions but scales exponentially, precluding realtime replanning [6].
Deep MARL offers a fundamentally different paradigm
by learning state-to-action mappings through trial-and-error
interaction. QMIX employs a monotonic mixing network to
factorize the global action-value function into per-agent local
utilities, enabling CTDE and excelling on purely cooperative
tasks [10]. MADDPG adopts a centralized critic–decentralized
actor framework, effectively handling mixed cooperativecompetitive settings [16]. MAPPO [20] achieves stable,
sample-efficient cooperative training, while QPLEX [21] introduces a duplex dueling network to overcome QMIX’s
monotonicity constraint.
Despite these advances, MARL deployment in air-ground
systems faces persistent challenges: sparse rewards impede
learning; sim-to-real transfer degrades under perceptual noise
and model mismatch [4]; and credit assignment among heterogeneous agents in non-stationary environments remains an
open problem [24].
V. C OOPERATIVE M OTION T RACKING C ONTROL
Model Predictive Control (MPC) has emerged as the
preferred solution for air-ground trajectory tracking owing to
its native handling of multi-input multi-output systems and
hard constraints. By solving a finite-horizon optimal control
problem at each timestep in a receding-horizon fashion, MPC
explicitly enforces velocity, acceleration, and inter-platform
collision-avoidance constraints. Nan et al. demonstrated nonlinear MPC’s effectiveness for quadrotor fault-tolerant control [22]; Romero et al. extended model predictive contouring
control to time-optimal flight [23], confirming MPC’s engineering viability for high-precision cooperative maneuvering.
The heavy online computation of nonlinear MPC—particularly
acute in multi-platform synchronous control—motivates ongoing work on lightweight formulations and neural network
approximations.
While classical MPC addresses execution-level robustness
effectively, task-level generalization across open-world scenarios remains beyond its scope, motivating the foundation-model
directions discussed next.

VI. F UTURE D IRECTIONS : F OUNDATION M ODELS AND
E MBODIED I NTELLIGENCE
Current air-ground systems have matured considerably
across the perception-to-control stack, yet fundamental limitations remain in task generalization and long-horizon reasoning
under open-world conditions. The integration of LLMs and
world models is reshaping the architectural paradigm toward
general-purpose embodied intelligence.
A. LLMs as High-Level Task Planners
LLMs enable air-ground systems to accept natural language
directives and decompose them into executable subtask sequences. Given “survey post-fire damage on the east side,” an
LLM decomposes this into UAV infrared scanning followed
by UGV close-range inspection [25]. The ELLMER framework shows that LLMs with retrieval-augmented generation
can dynamically revise plans across long, unpredictable task
horizons [26]. RT-2 vision-language-action models further
transfer web-scale knowledge to robotic manipulation, opening
a pathway for open-world semantic reasoning in air-ground
systems [27].
B. World Models and Embodied Intelligence
A world model serves as an agent’s internal simulator
of environment dynamics, enabling lookahead planning and
safety verification by imagining the consequences of cooperative strategies before real-world execution—dramatically
reducing trial-and-error costs [28]. DreamerV3 demonstrates
the feasibility of learning generalizable world representations across diverse physical domains [28], with considerable
value for air-ground applications. Looking ahead, systems
that fuse LLM task planning, world-model causal prediction,
and foundation-model perception will evolve from fixed-script
automation tools into embodied collaborative agents capable of
understanding intent and autonomously devising cooperative
strategies [29].
VII. C ONCLUSION
This paper has reviewed active collaborative perception and
planning for heterogeneous UAV-UGV systems along the full
perception–planning–decision–control chain. Three takeaways
emerge from this analysis. At the architectural level, CTDE
has become the dominant cooperative framework, with hierarchical MARL representing the most engineering-viable
path to distributed intelligence. In the perception domain,
task-driven active information acquisition has superseded passive fusion, though hybrid model-learning approaches remain
necessary for reliable deployment. On the planning side,
learned decision-making increasingly outperforms classical
optimization, but the sim-to-real gap demands co-design of
data-driven policies with model-based constraints. Bridging
this gap, alongside advances in cross-modal alignment and
emergent coordination in large-scale swarms, constitutes the
central challenge for the field. Looking further ahead, LLMs
and world models offer a transformative vision: air-ground
