Abstract— Over the last decades, we have experienced an
exponential growth in the use of Unmanned Aerial Vehicles
(UAVs) for inspection operations. However, the reduced flight
autonomy of electric powered UAVs can limit their applicability in long duration operations. In this paper, we propose
a marsupial system composed of a UAV and a Unmanned
Ground Vehicle (UGV). The system is made with off-the-shelf
components that can greatly extend the flight duration of
the UAV by powering it from the UGV using a tether, and
reducing the mobility limitations by the coordinated motion of
the system. We present the hardware design, making emphasis
in the decisions made and presenting different alternatives
depending on the application. Moreover, the software architecture for operating the device, based on the Robotic Operating
System (ROS) is presented. Finally, the system is validated in
experiments lasting more than one hour.
Keywords - Tethered robots, Long-endurance flight, Marsupial system

I. INTRODUCTION
In this paper, we focus on the use of a UAV for civil
infrastructure inspection. In this area, UAVs have become
a widespread and useful tool for applications as diverse as
structure assessment on buildings and bridges [1], power line
inspection, fire monitoring and extinguishing [2], [3] and
even sewer inspection [4].
However, there is a strong limitation that may prevent an
electric-powered UAV to perform an in-depth inspection of
structures, which is its flight time. Indeed, most commercial
platforms exhibit a flight duration well under half an hour,
which is usually measured in ideal circumstances, without
any additional payload (i.e. computing devices, sensors).
Also, the altitude and air temperature and density can affect
the flight duration of UAVs.
Some strategies can be employed to extend the flight
duration such as manual battery swapping, using swarms of
drones, and battery swapping stations. However, they came
at a cost of reducing the automation of the system, increasing
its complexity [5] or being still at an experimental level
[6]. Another alternative is to use tethered UAVs fixed to
a base station that supplies its power [7]. In fact, tethered

UAVs have gathered the attention of the community [8] with
applications as diverse as collaborative load transportation
[9] and airborne wind energy [10]. However, the use of a
tether fixed to a location may limit the UAV range to a great
extent, preventing it to safely reach some areas of interest.
Finally, Unmanned Ground Vehicles (UGVs), while capable
of operating during longer periods, are not suited to this task,
as they cannot reach inspection points located in high areas.
A marsupial multi-robot system [11] is a system in which
one robot carries and can deploy one or several other robots.
Some notable examples are the Oxpecter system [12], used
for mining inspection. Also, the Team CSIRO Data61 [13]
reached second place in the DARPA 2021 Subterranean
Challenge [14] with a Marsupial System. Last but not least,
the Mars 2020 rover and helicopter [15] which is being
used for planetary exploration on Mars. In this system. the
helicopter augments the capabilities of the rover, exploring
large areas faster than the rover, providing reconnaissance on
target locations and safe to traverse routes.
In this paper, we aim to devise, design and implement
a marsupial robotic system (see Fig. 1) able to perform
long-endurance inspection and surveillance tasks such as the
assessment of the structural health of civil buildings. In our
system, the UAV is linked to the UGV by means of a tether,
which is used to provide the UAV with power to operate
during long-endurance missions. Our design differs to some
of the designs in the literature in that it uses off-the-shelf
systems on the UAV, the UGV and the tether system. This

II. DESCRIPTION OF THE SYSTEM
We have designed a robotic system to carry out long-term
inspection tasks. This system is composed of a UGV, a UAV,
and a tether system (see Fig. 2). The main idea is to increase
the flight time of the UAV by providing power from the
UGV through the tether. Next, the main components of the
platforms are described:
A. Unmanned ground vehicle - UGV
We use a ARCO ground platform from ID-Mind1 [16],
which is a 4-wheeled holonomic robot with an independent
traction system (see Fig. 3a) that can work in holonomic or
differential configurations. The robot is able to navigate at
a maximum speed of 0.8 m/s. It carries two LiFePo 24V
batteries (for motors and electronics), with an autonomy
of 3 hours of operation. ARCO is a robot designed for
delivery of heavy loads in factories, making us able to equip
it with a specialized structure for the tether system directly
on the platform, as shown in Fig. 3b. The platform has a
maximum payload of over 100 kg, which is well above our
requirements.
To carry out the task we have properly equipped ARCO
with the necessary components to reach the marsupial configuration (see Fig. 4). We list in detail the components below:

B. Unmanned aerial vehicle
Regarding the aerial platform, the model used for the
experiments is the DJI Matrice 210 V2 (M210) (see Fig. 5).
We selected this platform because of the robustness of the
hardware and software, the support that the provider offers,
and its ROS support. The drone’s structure was adapted in
order to carry an i7 NUC computer and a LIDAR, as shown
Fig. 5. The main sensors and systems onboard the UAV are:
• DJI Matrice onboard sensors: This includes a single
GPS, an Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) and a barometric altimeter.
• 3D LIDAR sensor: An OS1-16 Ouster LIDAR is used
for the purpose of environment mapping, localization,
and odometry.
• WiFi module: It is used to increase the robustness, range
and performance of the WiFi connection at the UAV.
C. Tether System
We use the system LIGH-T V4 Tethered Station (see
Fig. 6), provided by the company Elistair2 . We selected this
system due to the support that provides the company and
its compatibility with the DJI M210 V2. The cable of the
system ends in an adapter, which is located in the drone
battery compartment to provide energy to the drone. The
output of this battery is 6S or 12S (battery in series) related
to LiPo 3.7 V. In our case, we must provide to the drone with
22.8 V, so, we must power the drone in a 6S configuration,
which means 6x3.7 V = 22.2 V. Finally, even though the UAV
draws its power from the Elistair system, it is also equipped
with a backup battery for the sake of safety.
The tether length is around 70 m. Besides, the system
works with 110 – 220 VAC (50 – 60 Hz) voltage input,
having a power consumption of 1200 W. This is the reason
why we use an inverter, to convert from VDC from the
battery pack to VAC needed for the system. The reel is
connected to a DC motor, which can be configured to
perform a constant tension to the cable ranging from 1N
to 3N.

The requirement defined for a long-duration flight in a
marsupial configuration is that the UAV must be capable of
carrying out a one hour inspection task with 60% of remaining battery, thus exceeding two hours of flight duration.
Figure 7 represents the interconnection scheme of the
power system, which consists of the following components:
• First, it is required that the inverter performs
220V/230V AC output to power the tether system.
Besides, because of the limitation of the space to install
the inverter on the ARCO, we must restrict the size.
Under these considerations we selected the Green Cell®
2000W/4000W inverter from 12V DC to 220V/230V
Pure Sine Wave.,with dimensions: 370 x 180 x 80 mm.
• Second, taking into account that the inverter inputs are
12 V, batteries were considered in this operating range.
We use LiFePo4 batteries given their safety in terms
of thermal and chemical stability, which significantly
reduces the risk of fire or explosion due to overload,
short circuits, or damage. In addition, they have a
long useful life, supporting a greater number of charge
and discharge cycles (typically more than 2000 cycles)
while maintaining a high capacity, which translates
into a longer useful life compared to other battery
technologies. Finally, they have a high energy density,
sufficient for most high-power and long-life applications
[17]. For these reasons, we selected the battery Joiry,
12.8 V, 150 Ah thus 1920 Wh), 328 x 172 x 214 mm.
Considering the power consumption of the tether system
(1200 W) we obtain a flight duration of 1 h and 36 min.
Given the demand of the tether system, in 1 hour 62.5% of
the battery will be consumed (1200/1920 × 100). To avoid
this, and reach the 60% charge of the batteries defined as a
requirement, we connect two batteries in parallel, for a total
of 300 Ah. In this way, we can extend the theoretical flight
duration to 3h and 12 min.
