Decentralized UAV-UGV Coordinated Navigation
under Connectivity Constraints

Mohammed Alwagait, Hakim Ghazzai, and Gianluca Setti
King Abdullah University of Science and Technology (KAUST), Thuwal, Saudi Arabia.
Abstract—This paper presents a novel decentralized navigation
framework for urban environments, integrating an Unmanned
Aerial Vehicle (UAV) with an Unmanned Ground Vehicle (UGV).
The system is designed such that the UAV aims to reach a
predefined destination to perform critical aerial tasks while the
UGV maintains proximity to support dynamic charging and
continuous operation. The navigation strategy employs a hybrid
path planning approach, combining a Probabilistic Roadmap
(PRM) with an A* search algorithm, further optimized by
path pruning for the UAV. In contrast, the UGV utilizes the
Dynamic Window Approach (DWA) for adaptive navigation. Both
unmanned vehicles operate while maintaining a safe distance to
ensure uninterrupted communication and prevent connectivity
loss. Validated through extensive simulations in ROS2 and
Gazebo, this framework enhances mission efficiency and enables
real-time coordination for effective decentralized navigation.
Index Terms—UAV, UGV, autonomous navigation, coordination, path planning.

I. I NTRODUCTION
Over the past decades, unmanned systems have experienced
remarkable advancements driven by improvements in computation, sensing, and control technologies. Unmanned Aerial
Vehicles (UAVs) have emerged as agile platforms capable of
rapid deployment and aerial reconnaissance, while Unmanned
Ground Vehicles (UGVs) offer robust endurance and higher
payload capacities. Despite their individual strengths, standalone UAVs and UGVs are often constrained by limitations
such as restricted energy capacity, mobility challenges, and
limited operational range. Recent trends in robotics research
have increasingly focused on integrating heterogeneous platforms into cooperative systems, a development that unlocks
new possibilities for tackling complex operational challenges
in dynamic, obstacle-rich environments. Applications of UAVUGV systems are diverse and include precision agriculture,
surveillance, and infrastructure inspection to search and rescue
operations in disaster-affected areas and environmental monitoring of hazardous terrains [1]–[4].
Recent research on integrated UAV-UGV systems demonstrates a broad range of applications and promising advancements in cooperative robotic platforms. Qin et al. [5] introduced a low-cost aerial–ground delivery system that harnesses
the complementary capabilities of UAVs and UGVs, emphasizing cost efficiency and operational flexibility in logistics.
Similarly, Singh et al. [6] explored UAV and UGV-assisted
path planning [7] for sensor data collection in precision agriculture, showcasing the potential to enhance data acquisition
and crop monitoring. In [8], the authors investigated cooperative 3D multi-object tracking by integrating multi-source
data fusion, significantly improving situational awareness and
tracking accuracy through the joint perspectives of aerial and
ground vehicles. Parallel to these developments, efforts have

been made to address the coordination and resource allocation
challenges inherent in UAV-UGV systems. Yan et al. [9] examined formation tracking using event-triggered communications,
ensuring robust and efficient coordination between platforms
under communication constraints. Moreover, Ma et al. [10]
proposed an adaptive depth graph neural network framework
for dynamic task allocation in complex environments, enhancing the system’s adaptability and operational robustness.
Together, these studies highlight the evolving capabilities of
integrated UAV-UGV systems and underscore a trend toward
more efficient, resilient, and versatile multi-agent robotic
platforms in both civilian and defense applications. Despite
these promising contributions, several limitations persist in
current UAV-UGV cooperative systems. Many studies, while
innovative in their respective application areas, have essentially
focused on the application aspect of the UAV-UGV system, developed centralized navigation approaches, or not considered
real-time coordination under communication limitations.
Building on the insights discussed, this paper proposes
a novel coordination UAV-UGV navigation framework in
obstacle-constrained environments where the UAV performs
mission-critical tasks and aiming at reaching pre-defined destinations, while the UGV functions as a mobile unit aiming
at following the UAV, e.g., mobile charging station. First, we
propose a hybrid global path planning strategy that combines
a Probabilistic Roadmap (PRM) with an A* search algorithm,
incorporating a path pruning mechanism to optimize the
planning process of the UAV. Second, we adopt the Dynamic
Window Approach (DWA) to continuously reposition the UGV
to stay in close proximity to the UAV. The coordinated
navigation is performed in a decentralized manner while
avoiding connectivity loss between the unmanned vehicles.
The framework is validated through simulations in ROS2
and Gazebo, demonstrating effective real-time coordinated
navigation. This approach diverges from traditional methods
that often handle aerial and ground vehicles separately or rely
on reinforcement learning solutions, which may compromise
accuracy and safety [11], [12].

III. UAV-UGV C OORDINATED NAVIGATION A PPROACH
This section presents the main methods enabling UAV-UGV
coordinated navigation in the obstacle-constrained environment where the UAV aims to reach a target location without
losing connectivity with the UGV. The latter aims to keep
close proximity with the flying unit.
A. UAV Navigation Framework
1) Global Path Planning for UAV: The shortest path on
the graph isn’t always the one on the environment because
the PRM can be sparse. [15] As a result, we include a
supplementary path simplification process to further optimize
the produced path. By determining if direct connections between intermediate waypoints are feasible without running into
barriers, this stage assesses whether the originally calculated
path can be reduced. A more direct route eliminates pointless
detours, cutting down on total trip distance and increasing
efficiency.
With the help of this two stage method PRM for roadmap
creation, A* for pathfinding, and a simplification check for
optimization the UAV is guaranteed to take the most effective
path while avoiding obstacles. PRM path is generated based
on N number of nodes in the 3d space. The Pure Pursuit Path

2) Path Following and Control for UAV: After the global
path has been determined, the UAV follows the computed
trajectory using a Pure Pursuit Path Solver [16]. This algorithm
calculates a series of target waypoints along the planned path
and continuously adjusts the UAV’s heading and velocity to
maintain smooth navigation. The Pure Pursuit method ensures
that the UAV maintains a stable path, avoiding sharp turns
or erratic movements. To further enhance trajectory tracking,
a PID Controller is integrated to regulate velocity and orientation. The PID controller plays a pivotal role in our UAV
control architecture by using continuous sensor feedback to
adjust the control inputs and maintain the desired flight path.
Specifically, the controller modulates the throttle, roll, pitch,
and yaw based on the UAV’s state, which is comprehensively
defined by the state vector in Equation (4). This state vector
provides detailed information about the vehicle’s position, orientation, and velocity, which is essential for the PID controller
to accurately compute the required adjustments. The final
control outputs from the PID controller are then applied to the
UAV’s dynamics model, executing the planned motion. This
multi-stage control framework enables precise and adaptive
UAV navigation, making it robust for real-world deployment
scenarios. The complete workflow for the UAV navigation is
depicted in Figure 2.
B. UGV Navigation Framework
Unlike the UAV, which follows a predefined global path,
we propose that the UGV dynamically adjusts its trajectory
using the NAV2 stack and the DWA planner [17]. This choice
allows the UGV to autonomously move while tracking the
UAV. In fact, the NAV2 stack provides a flexible and modular
navigation system that reacts to environmental changes. The
DWA planner, used within NAV2, is responsible for local path
planning and obstacle avoidance. DWA operates by evaluating
possible velocity commands within a defined window, selecting the most optimal movement that avoids obstacles while
keeping the UGV dynamically following the UAV without
requiring a strict predefined path. The UGV navigation process
follows these steps:
• Map Processing: The processed 3D PCD map is provided as input to NAV2, enabling the UGV to understand
the navigable space.
• Localization: The UGV continuously tracks its position
using sensor fusion (e.g., odometry and IMU).
