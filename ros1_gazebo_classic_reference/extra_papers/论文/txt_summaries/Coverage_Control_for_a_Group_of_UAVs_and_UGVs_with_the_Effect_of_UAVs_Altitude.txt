Coverage Control for a Group of UAVs and UGVs
with the Effect of UAVs Altitude

Abstract—This paper investigates coverage control for a group
of aerial and ground robots. Our analysis reveals a novel
cost function for placing the UAVs at the optimal height by
considering a realistic model for the robots. This function
provides a trade-off between the increasing coverage and reduction in quality of sensors which leads to a gradient-based
distributed controller. This controller makes the robots move
toward the optimal positions where they encode optimal coverage
and minimizes the overlap of the sensor ﬁeld of view (FoV).
Local optimal coverage is shown using the Lyapunov stability
theorem. Simulation results are also provided to demonstrate
the effectiveness of the deployment schemes.
Index Terms—Multi-agent systems, Coverage control, Voronoi
diagram, Quadrotor helicopter, UAVs, UGVs

I. I NTRODUCTION
A multi-agent system includes several agents interacting
with each other to accomplish a unique goal. Cooperative
control of multi-agent systems has numerous applications, one
of which is coverage control that allocates robots to provide
optimal solutions for monitoring based on a deﬁned objective
function. Applications of coverage control includes border
surveillance, identifying and exploring an unknown hazardous
environment, and search and recovery operations [1].
In [2], a coverage control using the Voronoi algorithm is
proposed. A similar method is used in [3] considering a real
model for robots with limited sensing range. The author in
[4] uses a distributed algorithm for coverage using robots with
different dynamics and capabilities. Using the multiplicatively
weighted Voronoi diagram, the robots minimize the introduced
cost function. Coverage in an unknown environment with barriers has been investigated in [5]. The robots avoid collisions
with existing obstacles by a propsed virtual density function.
In [6], two different robots, aerial and ground ones, cover
an unknown area. The ground robots are serving agents
responsible for coverage, and the aerial ones are explorer
robots that identify, explore, and detect the targets. [7] and [8]
utilize aerial robots equipped with cameras to monitor a twodimensional area. In [9], a multi-UAV system address coverage
in precision agriculture. The same problem is investigated in
[10], where the authors apply Deep Learning to control the
UAVs. The author in [11] covers an environment by a group of
quadrotors using locational optimization techniques. However,
it does not addresses the effect of UAVs’ altitude in coverage.

Deployment of multiple UAVs is of great importance in wireless networks. In this ﬁeld, UAVs act as aerial base stations
which feature desirable characteristics for rapid, on-demand,
or emergency communications. Authors in [12] address the
UAV deployment problem by analyzing the trade-offs between
crucial system parameters such as height, antenna beamwidth, and number of UAVs. In [13] and [14], UAVs emerge
with the internet of things (IoT) technology and wireless
sensor networks to collect data from devices that have limited
transmit power.
In this article, a heterogeneous multi-agent system is introduced in which robots have different nonholonomic dynamics. Several previous articles considered cooperation between
aerial and ground robots. The collaboration of a UAV and a
UGV, which have comparable motion patterns, but different
velocity constraints, is addressed in [15]. The authors propose
a novel moving center trajectory for the UAV with the help of
the UGV, which determines the center of trajectory. In [16],
a robotic system consisting of an aerial and ground robot is
investigated. The UAV acts as a leader, and the UGV follows
it to accomplish the search operations. In [17] and [18], the
UAVs are equipped with cameras that ﬂy above the UGVs
to inform the position, obstacles, and any possible danger.
In [19], the collaboration of UAVs and UGVs is applied for
information gathering and control in urban surveillance.
In this article, the aerial robot is a quadrotor helicopter, while
the wheeled robot has second-order differential dynamics with
omnidirectional sensors. With the mixture of these two types
of robots, we can take advantage of the aerial robot, which has
a higher speed and sensing range and the higher accuracy of
the ground robot to optimize the coverage. As the aerial robot
moves in three dimensions, the change in its altitude causes
different FoV. This fact can play an essential role in the quality
and scale of coverage. Section II reviews the mathematical
deﬁnition of the coverage problem and the solution, which is
based on the centroidal Voronoi partitions. Section III provides
the robots’ dynamics and their controllers. The coverage is
investigated in Section IV. A novel speciﬁc cost function is
derived in section V to ﬁnd the optimal altitude for each UAV.
The effectiveness of the proposed method is then evaluated
through numerical simulations shown in sub-section D.
