Abstract—The delivery service using unmanned aerial
vehicles (UAVs) and unmanned ground vehicles (UGVs)
collaboration has been a hot topic in the industry, and the lowcost UAV-UGV aerial-ground delivery system research has
significant practical significance. This study proposes a low-cost
aerial-ground delivery system for residential areas, based on
UAV-UGV collaboration. The system uses a 2D SLAM enabled
UGV for precise ground transportation and a quadrotor UAV
equipped with SVO for high-altitude delivery of small and
medium-sized packages. Both the UGV and the UAV are
developed based on the Robot Operating System (ROS). This
study comparing energy consumption and time taken under
different operating modes and visual odometers respectively..
The delivery process is divided into three stages and a visualmaximization-based SVO fusion method is proposed for the UAV
search in case of target loss. Experimental results show that the
proposed system is effective, accurate, timely, and has high
practical value.

I. INTRODUCTION

Considering the generally flat ground in residential
environments, multi-line LiDAR is more expensive and
computationally demanding than single-line LiDAR. Shen et al.
conducted an in-depth analysis and comparative study
of LiDAR SLAM algorithms for mobile robots [11], finding
that the Cartographer scheme has stronger real-time
performance in low-cost environments.

The rapid growth of logistics demand and consumer
demand for faster delivery speeds has led to the proposal of
using UAVs for goods delivery by companies such as Amazon,
Google, Meituan, and JD.com [1]. Successful cases have
shown that small parcels can be directly delivered to rural area
consumers' balconies using GPS and the UAV's height
limitlessness [2]. Hence, efficient and low-cost UAV delivery
systems have important practical significance and application
prospects.

R. Xuan et al. summarized five forms of coordination for
delivery: joint, parallel, UGV/UAV-supported, and mixed
delivery [12]. The topology of the problem can be abstracted
into a TSP model for research. M. Shan et al. developed a twostage solution method to optimize total cost for truck-UAV
logistics based on a "maximum weight judgment method" and
improved simulated annealing algorithm [13]. For multiple
loop deliveries, A. M. El-Adle et al. proposed a Variable
Neighborhood Search (VNS) heuristic algorithm to solve the
TSP-D and a two-stage strengthening scheme used by VNS
[14].

Although Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) have shown
good performance in many fields, their use of GPS to obtain
latitude and longitude information is prone to signal failure due
to the dense construction of buildings. Furthermore, due to
their weight limitations, using UGVs for delivery over the
same distance is the lowest cost option. Therefore, researching
the coordinated delivery of UAVs and UGVs is of great
significance [3].As research into unmanned aerial vehicleunmanned ground vehicle systems provides support for our
work [4].

II. SYSTEM FRAMEWORK
A. Hardware Components
In order to explore more cost-effective and faster delivery
solutions, we initially propose a combination of unmanned
aerial vehicles (UAVs) and utility ground vehicles (UGVs),
and then compare it with other options. To achieve cost
minimization, our UAV hardware configuration includes an
Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) for controlling the UAV's
attitude, a main control board and motion control board, a
quadrotor UAV, and a forward-facing monocular camera
(used for target search). We also use a wifi module to
communicate with the UGV, connecting to wifi through a selfbuilt hotspot for communication.

In the UGV part, we use a Mecanum wheel vehicle with
an Ackermann structure chassis and a single-line lidar for
environment perception and modeling. The UGV's main
control board employs an Intel I5 processor for system
efficiency and stability. For cost-effectiveness, we selected the
EAI G1 high-precision lidar for the single-line lidar hardware
device. During delivery, the ROS system is used for
autonomous UGV navigation, enabling precise positioning and
path planning. To ensure efficient coordination between the
UGV and UAV, we equip a wireless network
card for bidirectional communication between the two systems.
In the UAV part, we utilize a forward-facing monocular
RGB camera as the sole observation sensor. Its main function
is to detect the delivery area location and establish a local
map during ascent and return phases. Using SVO visual
odometry technology, the camera also estimates depth via
triangulation, enabling obstacle avoidance. A Wi-Fi module
facilitates information exchange and command reception with
the UGV, and the relative pose relationship between the UAV
and UGV is transmitted through this module, ensuring a
smooth UGV return.

The UAV hardware includes an IMU for attitude control,
main and motion control boards for flight control and
navigation, and a monocular camera for visual perception and
recognition. The UGV hardware comprises a main control
board, single-line lidar for high-precision perception and
modeling, and a Mecanum wheel vehicle with an Ackermann
structure for good maneuverability and versatility in indoor and
outdoor environments.

III. IMPLEMENTATION
A. Delivery Area Detection
When selecting a delivery area detection method, we
prioritized low cost and high detection speed. We evaluated
various mainstream target detection algorithms, including
traditional algorithms such as SIFT, SURF, and deep learning
methods such as RCNN, SSD, and YOLO. After conducting
experiments and comparisons, we found that YOLO, SSD, and
traditional target detection algorithms have better stability and
real-time performance in solving the problem of instance
detection. However, deep learning-based algorithms require
significant upfront preparation and consume computational
resources, and the characteristics of different household types
in residential areas are inconsistent, increasing the difficulty of
detection tasks. In comparison, traditional visual
methods utilizing templates for target detection also
demonstrate remarkable performance. Ultimately, we chose
template-based traditional target detection methods to complete
this task.
We selected the WhyCon template from the optional visual
markers because of its seamless integration with the ROS
system, fast running speed, and long detection distance.
Experimental results verified the correctness of our choice, as it
reduced costs without compromising running speed. By
selecting appropriate algorithms and templates, we efficiently
implemented
target
detection
and
tracking,
enabling autonomous navigation and positioning of the system.
Our choices also considered the balance between cost and
performance, improving the system's scalability and
practicality.

B. 2D Navigation Algorithms
Accurate map construction is a crucial step in the field of
SLAM, as it ensures navigation accuracy. When selecting a 2D
SLAM algorithm for our system, we carefully weighed the
advantages and disadvantages of various algorithms, given our
use of a single-line lidar. F. Lu et al. [15] have presented
several feasible solutions, including Gmapping, Cartographer,
Hector, and others, each with unique strengths and weaknesses.
Ultimately, we selected the Cartographer algorithm for our
system.

For packages that are heavy for delivery by UAV, the
system adopts a method of notifying the recipient in advance to
pick up the package at a designated location. During the UAV
delivers small packages, the UGV will deliver the large
packages to the designated location, which increases the
difficulty of the UAV returning to the UGV. Fortunately, our
UAV uses an application that relies on visual odometry (SVO)
and can construct maps of the surrounding environment during
the end delivery process. In addition, the UAV and UGV need
to communicate over short distances to achieve map sharing
based on the UAV's local map. Through coordinate
transformation, we can build the coordinate transformation
relationship between the UAV and the UGV, thereby ensuring
that the UAV can successfully "return".

Our selection of a SLAM algorithm was based on
considerations of applicable scenarios and hardware
requirements. GMAPPING lacks loop closure detection and is
not suitable for constructing large scenes, while Hector has
high requirements for laser radar and does not utilize odometry
information. Karto is suitable for large-scale environments, but
Cartographer, which utilizes odometry and IMU, has global
and local maps, backend optimization, and low cumulative
error, even with low-cost laser radar. Therefore, we chose
Cartographer for map building, enabling efficient map
construction and autonomous navigation. This selection
ultimately supports the successful realization of autonomous
control and positioning for our UAV-UAG joint delivery
system.

In our system, we use SVO for high-precision UAV
positioning and map construction. Coordinate transformation
technology enables effective communication and coordination
between the UAV and UGV, improving delivery efficiency
and accuracy. If the recipient marker is not detected during
delivery, the UAV performs a target search based on the
maximum parallel view of the plane and returns home if
unsuccessful. We utilize visual sensors and deep
learning for obstacle avoidance functionality during UAV
movement. Additionally, a maximum parallel view of the plane
algorithm is used for target search and positioning.
